# Alternative One-Shot Ideas

## 1. The Last Cart to Falkenbreach

**Premise:** The party is on the last supply cart heading out of a dying mining town before it's evacuated. They're not heroes arriving — they're people leaving. But the cart keeps breaking down, the road keeps changing, and the forest doesn't want them to leave.

**Genre:** Folk horror / road trip survival
**Length:** 3-4 hours
**Players:** 3-5, levels 3-5

### Structure — 5 stops, not 7 scenes

Each time the cart "breaks down," something forces a choice:

1. **The Ferryman** — A bridge is washed out. An old woman offers passage on her raft for "a memory, not a coin." One player must give up a backstory memory (you decide if it's real). She knows things she shouldn't.

2. **The Village That Wasn't There Yesterday** — A hamlet appears off the road. Warm fires, full tavern, friendly people. It's a comfort trap. If the party stays past midnight, they forget why they were leaving. Escaping requires realizing something is wrong — no combat needed, just suspicion.

3. **The Thing on the Road** — A massive stag with a human face blocks the path. It won't attack unless attacked. It asks a riddle. Wrong answer = it stamps and the forest shifts again. Right answer = it walks away, and one player sees it wasn't a stag at all.

4. **The Passenger** — Someone is in the cart who wasn't there before. They claim they've always been traveling with the party. They're helpful. They might be a ghost. They might be a god. They won't explain. When the party reaches Falkenbreach, they're gone — but the innkeeper remembers them from 40 years ago.

5. **Falkenbreach, Or Close Enough** — The town appears. But the innkeeper is the same woman from the ferry. The blacksmith was the stag. The town was helping them leave. Or keeping them. The final moment: do they stay, or keep walking?

### Why this is different

- **No murder, no villain, no boss fight** — the antagonist is the road itself
- **Player choice matters more than player skill** — every stop is a decision, not a skill check
- **No whodunit** — the mystery is atmospheric, not evidential
- **The Passenger NPC**: a roleplaying gift that writes itself. Your theater kid player will lose their mind
- **Ambiguous ending by design** — the party debates what happened on the ride home IRL

### The twist you can seed

Name-drop the mining town they're fleeing at session start. When they describe Falkenbreach later, it has the same mayor, the same fountain, the same everything. They didn't leave. The town just *looked* different for a night so they'd stop trying.

## 2. The Black Salt Auction

**Premise:** The party has been given 1,000 gold and one job: attend an underground auction held once a decade in a flooded cathedral, and buy something — anything. They don't know what's for sale. They don't know that the auctioneer is a lich who collects the losers as payment. They have one hour of real time to spend their gold or die.

**Genre:** Competitive dark fantasy / resource puzzle
**Length:** 3-4 hours
**Players:** 4, levels 4-6

### The Auction — 5 lots, real-time bidding

You run this with a literal 60-minute timer visible to the table. Each lot takes ~10 minutes of bidding and description. Between lots, the party can explore the cathedral, talk to other bidders, and piece together what's happening.

**Lot 1 — A sealed wooden box.** The auctioneer won't say what's inside. Other bidders are frantic for it. *It contains a scroll of Resurrection. The party won't know unless they win it and open it.*

**Lot 2 — A living goblin in chains.** It's screaming that it's a cursed prince. *It's telling the truth. If freed, it becomes a powerful ally. Other bidders want it as a ritual component.*

**Lot 3 — A map drawn on human skin.** *It shows the cathedral itself — specifically, the escape route. Nobody realizes this. It looks worthless.*

**Lot 4 — A sword that whispers.** Mechanically strong, but the whispering spirit makes the wielder increasingly paranoid. Each round of combat, the DM whispers a lie to that player. *The other 3 players have to decide if they trust this person still.*

**Lot 5 — "The house takes the remaining gold from every bidder who hasn't won a lot."** This is announced with 10 minutes left. If you haven't spent your money, you lose it *and* you're marked as payment.

### Running the Bidding

Each lot works like this (~10 minutes per lot):

1. Describe the item dramatically (or vaguely, like the sealed box)
2. Open bidding at a low number (e.g., "100 gold")
3. The players confer — do they want it? Can they afford it? Do they pool gold or keep reserves? Is the goblin lying? Is the box a trap?
4. An NPC raises. Roleplay them in one sentence: *The noble in the corner raises two fingers. "Two hundred."* You only need 3-4 NPC bidders per lot, each with a clear personality and spending limit.

**NPC Bidders:**

| NPC | Personality | Budget | What They Want |
|-----|------------|--------|---------------|
| Lord Kessel | Arrogant, raises by large jumps | 800g | The sword, the map |
| The Whisperer | Hooded, never speaks above a whisper, always bids last | 1,200g | The box, the goblin |
| Three Merchants | Share gold, always debate loudly before bidding | 600g each, pooled | Anything profitable |
| A hollow-eyed woman | Desperate, bids everything she has | 400g | The goblin (she's its mother) |

**The real decision for the players isn't "do I raise?" — it's which lot do they commit to.** They can't win everything. Gold is finite. If they blow 700g on the box, they can't outbid the Whisperer on the goblin.

**The 60-minute timer is wall clock time.** It's visible. When it hits 10 minutes left, announce Lot 5 and the stakes change completely.

### After the gavel falls

The auction ends. The locked doors open. The winners keep their items (which now shape the escape). The losers? The lich collects. The party must fight their way out through the cathedral's flooded crypts, past other desperate bidders who are willing to kill for what the party bought.

- Won the **box**? Someone's getting resurrected mid-fight.
- Freed the **goblin prince**? You have a spellcaster ally.
- Bought the **map**? You know the way out — but so does everyone who sees you reading it.
- Have the **sword**? You're the strongest fighter, but you might attack your friends.
- Spent **nothing**? You're broke, marked, and running.

### Why this is different

- **No investigation phase** — you're not solving anything, you're *buying* your future
- **The timer is real** — 60 minutes on a clock creates urgency the table physically feels
- **Every player debates every bid** — natural roleplay from money anxiety
- **The combat is shaped by shopping** — your purchases ARE your build for the finale
- **4 players = perfect bidding tension** — can you afford to outbid? Do you pool gold? Can you trust the person holding the sword?

## 3. The Loop

**Premise:** The party wakes up in the village of Ashen at dawn. Birds singing. Market stalls opening. A kid drops a basket of apples in the street. By sunset, a dragon destroys the village. Everyone dies. Then they wake up at dawn again. Same birds. Same kid. Same apples. They're stuck in a time loop, and they have as many tries as they need to figure out how to save Ashen — or whether it can be saved at all.

**Genre:** Groundhog Day meets D&D
**Length:** 3-4 hours (5-7 loops)
**Players:** 4, levels 3-5

### How the loop works

- **Each loop is one in-game day (dawn to sunset, roughly 12 hours).** You track time roughly: morning, midday, afternoon, evening, sunset.
- **When they die or the dragon arrives, reset.** Players keep all knowledge. HP, spell slots, and items reset to their starting state.
- **NPCs do the same things every loop unless the party changes something.** The blacksmith always goes to the well at noon. The mayor always holds council at 2pm. The dragon always comes from the east at sunset.
- **The loop is the puzzle.** There is no investigation scene. The entire day is the investigation. Every loop they learn more: where the dragon nests, why it's attacking, what the village is hiding, who knows more than they're saying.

### Loop types and what they look like

**Loop 1 — Confusion.** They don't know the dragon is coming. They're shopping, talking, exploring. Sunset hits. Dragon. Death. Total shock. This is the "oh no" loop. Let it be quick. Let it be unfair.

**Loop 2 — Panic.** They try to warn people. Nobody believes them. "A dragon? Don't be ridiculous." They can't convince the village in one day. This teaches them that information alone isn't enough — they need proof, trust, and a plan.

**Loop 3-4 — Experimentation.** This is where it gets fun. They start testing. What if we go east at dawn? They find the dragon's ridge. What if we rob the mayor's office? They find letters about a deal gone wrong. What if we just leave town? The loop still resets — they can't outrun it. The village needs saving. What if we kill the dragon? It's too strong in a straight fight. What if we talk to it?

**Loop 5-6 — Execution.** They have a plan. Maybe they've convinced the blacksmith to forge weapons overnight (they can give him the design this loop since they know what works). Maybe they've found the cave where the dragon sleeps and can ambush it before sunset. Maybe they've learned the village's sin and need to make amends. This is the satisfying loop where everything clicks.

### The secret: the village made a deal

Ashen traded its firstborn children to a dragon 200 years ago for protection during a war. The war ended. The deal didn't. The dragon returns every generation to collect, and the village sends a child away and calls it "apprenticeship." This generation, a mother refused. She hid her daughter. The dragon came anyway. The loop is her grief, turned into a curse. She's the old woman who sells flowers in the square. She doesn't know she's doing it.

### Seeding Ilsa (the mother) across loops

Ilsa doesn't know she's the cause. So you can't have her monologue about it. The hints have to be observational.

**Loop 1-2 (they won't notice these, but you describe them):**

- When the child drops apples in the square, a flower woman laughs and helps pick them up. Sweet scene. Doesn't seem important. She's just set dressing.
- There's a mural in the town hall showing the village's founding. A dragon is in it, but painted over. You only mention it if they look at the wall. Most won't on loop 1.

**Loop 3 (they start paying attention to patterns):**

- Ilsa is the *only* NPC who changes her behavior between loops without the party interacting with her first. She's always in the square at dawn, but by midday she's sometimes at the church, sometimes at the graveyard, sometimes standing at the east gate *staring east* (toward the dragon's ridge). If a player points this out, that's the thread.
- If they talk to her, she's kind but distracted. She mentions her daughter is "away at apprenticing, like all the village children do." If they ask around, they learn the village has a tradition of sending firstborns to "apprentice" in the east. Nobody questions it. Nobody knows where exactly.

**Loop 4 (if they've found the mayor's letters):**

- The mayor's letters mention "the arrangement" and "the payment due this generation." They also mention Ilsa by name. The letter says she refused. The mayor wrote: *"If Ilsa won't give the child, we must consider alternatives. The dragon won't wait."*
- If they confront Ilsa with this, she breaks down. She says her daughter is hidden. She won't say where. And then she says something she shouldn't know: *"I've had this conversation before. I've had it so many times."* She doesn't remember the loops consciously, but her grief does. That line is the tell.

**Loop 5 (the reveal):**

- If they find where Ilsa hid her daughter (the church cellar, behind a false wall), the girl is there, scared, drawing pictures of a dragon over and over. She says: *"Mama says the dragon comes because I'm hiding. She says if I come out, it stops."* That's the curse mechanized — the girl believes it's her fault, and Ilsa's grief made that belief real enough to trap the day.

### Fighting the dragon

The dragon is too strong on loop 1. Full stop. If they fight it, they die. That's fine — they loop. But they learn things across loops that make killing it possible:

**What they learn by exploring:**

- **Loop 3:** If they go east at dawn, they find the dragon's ridge. It sleeps until sunset. So they learn: it's vulnerable in the morning. They also find old claw marks on the rock — it's been coming here a long time, on a schedule.
- **Loop 4:** If they explore the abandoned watchtower on the north hill, they find a journal from a ranger who died 20 years ago. The journal describes the dragon's flight patterns, its weak spot (a gap in the scales under its left jaw where an old spear wound never healed), and that it flies low over the mill at midday to drink from the river. That's a window.
- **Loop 4-5:** They can prep the village across loops. Tell the blacksmith exactly what to forge (they know it works because they tested it last loop). Position the mayor's guards where they need them. Set the trap at the mill. Give the priestess a scroll list they know the dragon will fail a save against because they saw it cast the same spells last loop.

**The fight itself on the final loop:**

They've done 5 loops. They know the dragon's schedule, its weak spot, its spell list, and its approach angle. The blacksmith has dragon-piercing arrows ready because they gave him the design two loops ago. The guards are in position. They're at the mill at midday when it flies low. It's still hard. It's a dragon. But they've *earned* the knowledge. The loop doesn't just make them more informed — it makes them tactically dangerous. They've fought this dragon 4 times. They know its patterns better than it knows itself.

### The three endings

All three are valid. The party chooses which one they earn:

- **Diplomatic:** Help Ilsa and her daughter break the curse. Confront the village's sin. The dragon doesn't come. The day doesn't reset.
- **Combat:** Kill the dragon with prep earned across loops. The threat is gone. The day doesn't reset because the danger has passed.
- **Third option:** Confront Ilsa, learn the truth, then go to the dragon and renegotiate the deal on the village's terms. The dragon is intelligent — it made a contract. It might accept a different payment.

### Why this is different

- **You literally can't lose** — which means the party takes risks they'd never take in a normal one-shot. That's the point. Let them be reckless. Let them fail spectacularly. The loop catches them.
- **The dragon is the timer, not the enemy** — every loop has a natural deadline. No DM needed to create urgency. Sunset comes.
- **4 players = 4 experiments per loop** — split the party intentionally. "You check the east road, you talk to the mayor, you find the blacksmith, I'll watch the old woman." Each loop covers more ground.
- **The puzzle is the whole day, not one scene** — they're not searching for clues in a room. They're learning an entire day.
- **The ending is earned** — when they finally save Ashen, they've lived that day 5-7 times. They know every NPC by name. They know which alley is safe. The sunrise at the end means something because they've seen it destroyed.
- **Combat solves it too** — this isn't a "violence bad" one-shot. If your table wants to fight, the loops make that possible and satisfying. They earned the intel. They earned the prep. Let them kill the dragon.
